Modelling Intravascular
Drug Concentration

A two-compartment pharmacokinetic study grounded in the realities of sepsis treatment across sub-Saharan Africa.

Why This Matters

Every hour costs lives.

Every hour a sepsis patient waits for effective antibiotics, their chance of survival drops. Getting the dose wrong — too little or too much — is not a minor error. In African hospitals, it happens constantly, and the consequences are irreversible.

In 2017, an estimated 48.9 million sepsis cases were recorded globally, with nearly one in five of all deaths worldwide attributable to the condition. Across sub-Saharan Africa, in-hospital mortality for sepsis patients reaches 38% — more than double the rate seen in high-income countries.

One in four critically ill patients in African hospitals risks receiving an antibiotic dose that is either too low to clear the infection or high enough to cause permanent organ damage.

Gentamicin: powerful, affordable, and dangerously narrow.

The antibiotic most commonly used to treat sepsis in Ghanaian and African hospitals is gentamicin, an intravenous drug that is affordable, available, and effective. But gentamicin operates within a dangerously narrow therapeutic window.

Too little and the bacteria survive — the infection worsens and resistance can develop. Too much and the drug attacks the kidneys and destroys hearing, leaving damage irreversible. In hospitals with limited laboratory monitoring, toxic levels can accumulate silently, long before any clinical warning sign appears.

The Narrow Window Problem
The gap between the concentration required to kill bacteria and the concentration at which gentamicin begins to damage the kidneys and inner ear is small. In settings with limited monitoring, this gap is routinely crossed undetected.

Critically ill patients with sepsis often have altered volume distribution due to fluid shifts, impaired kidney function that slows drug elimination, and compromised organ perfusion. A dose that is therapeutic in a healthy patient may be sub-therapeutic — or toxic — in a septic patient.

Dosing by averages that cannot account for individuals.

The human body does not treat every patient the same way. For decades, clinicians across Ghana and Africa have had no reliable mathematical tool to predict where the drug is in the body at any moment — how much is in the bloodstream, how much has reached the tissues, and how fast it is being cleared.

IV Dosing Blind Spots

Once a drug enters the body, its distribution becomes invisible without mathematical modelling. Clinicians could only observe effects after the fact.

Individual Physiology Varies

Critically ill patients distribute and eliminate drugs differently. Population averages and weight tables cannot account for this variation.

Limited Monitoring Capacity

African hospitals often lack the laboratory infrastructure to measure real-time drug concentrations — making predictive models even more essential.

A mathematical model of the body as two connected spaces.

This project investigates the mathematical modelling of intravascular drug concentration using a two-compartment pharmacokinetic model — a framework that treats the body as two connected spaces: the bloodstream and the tissues.

By expressing how drugs move between these spaces as a system of differential equations, the model makes it possible to predict drug concentration at any point in time after administration, for any patient whose physiological parameters are known.

The project further evaluates three numerical methods for solving these equations computationally, identifying which method produces predictions accurate enough to be trusted in clinical decision-making.

By grounding this mathematical analysis in the lived reality of sepsis treatment in Ghana and across Africa, this project bridges the gap between abstract modelling and concrete human outcomes — the kind of tool that, properly understood and applied, can help ensure the right dose reaches the right place at the right time.
